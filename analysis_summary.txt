SECTION 2: GRADIENT VS CATEGORICAL R² COMPARISON
========================================================

  Model 1 - WER ~ L1 Background (categorical)
    R² = 0.6040  (explains 60.4% of WER variance)

  Model 2 - WER ~ Mean Accent Strength (gradient)
    R² = 0.0776  (explains  7.8% of WER variance)

  Model 3 - WER ~ L1 + Mean Accent Strength (full)
    R² = 0.7876  (explains 78.8% of WER variance)

FINDING: Categorical L1 background explains SUBSTANTIALLY
MORE variance in WER than gradient accent strength.

  Delta R² (gradient minus categorical) = -0.5264

  In other words, knowing which L1 group a speaker belongs to
  (Arabic, Chinese, Hindi, etc.) is a much stronger predictor
  of their ASR word error rate than their individually rated
  accent strength score.

  This suggests that ASR errors are driven more by systematic,
  language-specific phonological patterns (shared within an L1
  group) than by the overall perceptual "heaviness" of an
  individual's accent as rated by human listeners.

  Importantly, the full model combining both predictors reaches
  R² = 0.7876, an incremental gain of +0.1836 over the
  categorical-only model. This means that even after accounting
  for L1 group, individual accent strength ratings add
  meaningful additional predictive power.

SECTION 3: CORRELATION (ACCENT STRENGTH vs WER)
========================================================

  Pearson r  = 0.2785
  p-value    = 0.1875  (not significant, n.s.)
  R²         = 0.0776
  95% CI:    [-0.1407, 0.6131]
  N          = 24

INTERPRETATION:
  The correlation between mean accent strength and WER is
  positive (r = 0.28), indicating that speakers rated as
  having stronger accents tend to have higher ASR error rates.
  However, this relationship does not reach statistical
  significance (p = 0.19), and the confidence interval spans
  zero, meaning we cannot rule out a null relationship.

  The modest and non-significant correlation is consistent
  with the model comparison results: accent strength as
  perceived by human raters accounts for only about 8% of
  the variance in ASR performance. The remaining variance is
  better explained by L1-specific factors not fully captured
  by a general accent strength scale.

  Note: The small sample size (N = 24) limits statistical
  power. A larger sample may be needed to detect a reliable
  gradient effect if one exists.


========================================================
SECTION 4: KEY FINDING SUMMARY
========================================================

MAIN QUESTION:
  Does gradient accent strength explain WER better than
  categorical L1 background?

ANSWER: No. Categorical L1 background is the stronger predictor.

EVIDENCE:
  1. L1 category alone explains 60.4% of WER variance
     (ANOVA: F(5,18) = 5.49, p = 0.003, η² = 0.60).

  2. Gradient accent strength explains only 7.8% of WER
     variance and the correlation is not significant
     (r = 0.28, p = 0.19).

  3. The full model (L1 + accent) explains 78.8%, meaning
     accent strength adds ~18 percentage points of variance
     on top of L1 category — a meaningful contribution, but
     not a replacement for categorical L1 information.

IMPLICATION:
  ASR systems appear to struggle most with specific L1
  phonological transfer patterns rather than with general
  accent "heaviness." Language-specific training data and
  acoustic model adaptation by L1 background may be more
  effective than targeting speakers with heavy accents in
  general. Individual accent strength may still be a useful
  secondary predictor once L1 is controlled for.

  * p < 0.05 (significant within Hindi group)

  Notable observations:
  - Vietnamese shows the largest WER range (Δ=0.18) and
    highest mean WER (0.463), suggesting the most within-
    group heterogeneity in ASR difficulty.
  - Hindi shows a significant negative within-group
    correlation (r = -0.955, p = 0.045), meaning higher-
    rated Hindi speakers had LOWER WER — a counterintuitive
    finding that may warrant further investigation.
  - Arabic and Spanish show strong positive within-group
    trends (r ~0.93-0.95) approaching but not reaching
    significance, likely due to small n (4 per group).
  - Korean shows the lowest overall mean WER (0.317) and
    the lowest mean accent ratings (1.85), consistent with
    better ASR performance for Korean-accented speakers.

  The within-group variation reinforces that accent strength
  adds explanatory value beyond L1 category alone for most
  language groups.
